## Design Context

### Users
This product serves two equally important audiences:

- Local Phoenix and Tempe customers deciding whether to stop by a pop-up, place an order, or call quickly from their phone.
- Families, hosts, and higher-end event clients evaluating whether K.Lou's is trustworthy, polished, and memorable enough to cater an important event.

The core jobs are:

- Understand what K.Lou's offers within seconds.
- See whether the food feels genuinely authentic and worth seeking out.
- Find the current location or menu without friction.
- Call fast for immediate questions.
- Request catering with confidence for events ranging from family gatherings to premium private or corporate occasions.

Users should leave with appetite, trust, and a sense that the business is both warm and professionally credible.

### Brand Personality
The brand should feel:

- Authentic
- Welcoming to families
- Appealing to high-end clients

In practice, that means the tone should feel grounded, generous, and polished without drifting into corporate sterility or faux-luxury theater. It should suggest real Louisiana hospitality and real craft, not trend-chasing branding.

### Aesthetic Direction
The interface should avoid AI-generic card UIs and avoid leaning on Playfair Display as a shortcut for "premium."

Preferred direction:

- Editorial hospitality, not dashboard marketing.
- Food-forward and tactile, with strong photography and confident typography.
- Warm and inviting enough for families, but composed and refined enough for premium catering buyers.
- Structured around rhythm, contrast, and page composition rather than endless repeated cards.

Anti-direction:

- Generic AI landing page grids
- Repetitive card stacks for every section
- Overused luxury-serif treatment
- Template-looking "premium" styling that could belong to any restaurant

Unless a specific feature proves otherwise, design decisions should bias toward a light-first experience with rich, grounded accents, because the product is most likely browsed in daytime, mobile, and food-discovery contexts.

### Design Principles
- Lead with appetite and trust. The interface should sell the food and the hospitality before it sells the layout.
- Design for two modes at once. Quick local browsing and high-trust catering evaluation must both feel natural in the same system.
- Warmth over trendiness. The site should feel human, family-friendly, and confident rather than flashy or artificially "luxury."
- Break the card-grid reflex. Use fewer containers, stronger section composition, and more intentional hierarchy.
- Premium means clarity and restraint. Higher-end clients should see evidence of quality, professionalism, and confidence, not decorative excess.
- Every call to action should reduce hesitation. "Call," "Find us," and "Book catering" should always feel obvious, distinct, and immediate.
